1.1 Why ERP architectural constraints show up in the same ways

Most ERPs (1C, SAP, Microsoft Dynamics, Odoo) look stable at the start: little data, simplified processes, rare exceptions, ‚Äúthin‚Äù integrations. Architectural constraints are almost invisible at this stage.

Problems appear later ‚Äî when the company starts changing rules. The reason is not a ‚Äúbad system‚Äù, but the fact that typical ERPs are built around similar trade-offs: an object model, a separate reporting layer, split logic (forms/server/integrations), and limitations of the query layer and bulk operations. As data and exceptions grow, these trade-offs produce the same symptoms regardless of vendor.

1.2 What counts as a ‚Äúrule change,‚Äù not a customization

A ‚Äúrule change‚Äù is not ‚Äúadd a field‚Äù and not ‚Äúbuild a new report.‚Äù It‚Äôs a change that simultaneously:

  • changes transaction behavior (validity, posting, statuses, constraints);

  • changes control (locks, approvals, conditional prohibitions);
  • must not break reporting and integrations (CRM/WMS/marketplaces/BI).

If the platform doesn‚Äôt hold this as a single model (data + calculations + control), the rule inevitably gets implemented in multiple places. Then the cost is driven not by how complex the formula is, but by how many layers must be changed and verified in sync.
