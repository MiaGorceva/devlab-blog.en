Mini-cases: the changes that look small but cost real money

Case 1 ‚Äî discounts with 5 conditions (and they change weekly)

The business asks for pricing rules based on client tier, region, product group, volume, payment terms ‚Äî and exceptions. In many ERP setups, this turns into a chain of special cases. Reports drift. Managers ‚Äúwork around‚Äù the system.

A change-friendly design keeps pricing as explicit rule logic ‚Äî consistent across documents, calculations, and reporting.

Case 2 ‚Äî approvals that reflect real management (not the UI)

‚ÄúIf amount > X ‚Üí CFO approval. If the client is new ‚Üí risk review. If overdue > N days ‚Üí accounting approval.‚Äù These are normal operational rules. This is the founder moment: ‚ÄúWhy does the report show profit but cash says otherwise?‚Äù The root cause is usually scattered logic: partial cost allocation, inconsistent discount handling, logistic costs applied elsewhere, retroactive adjustments, or ‚Äúre-post to fix‚Äù.

‚ÄúJust add a new document type‚Äù sounds easy. In practice, a new entity must participate in reporting, pricing, approvals, KPIs, constraints, and audit logic. If logic is scattered, every new entity triggers a cascade of changes.

Not ‚Äúa chatbot in ERP‚Äù. The useful AI story is simpler: business logic should be readable enough that an analyst ‚Äî assisted by AI (acting like a junior engineer) ‚Äî can implement and validate many changes without becoming hostage to a single developer or integrator.
