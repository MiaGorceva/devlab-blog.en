Top 12 Open-Source ERP & Business Systems (That People Actually Use)

“Open-source ERP” is an umbrella term. For some teams it means installing a ready-made system and configuring modules. For others, it means using a platform to assemble business logic around their own processes. This article focuses on practical differences: what each approach does well, where it starts to hurt, and which types of teams usually succeed with it.

How this list was selected (without marketing fluff)

I’m using a blend of signals: community visibility (including GitHub popularity where it’s relevant), long-term presence, and “can teams actually implement this without rewriting their company around the tool”. You’ll see projects that show up in well-known ERP roundups, plus developer-validated picks from star-based lists.

Quick map: 3 categories you should not mix up

  • Ready ERP suites: install, configure, run (best when you need “ERP now”).
  • ERP platforms: build/extend modules fast (best when you have unique processes).
  • Business-system builders: low-code foundations to assemble ERP-like apps (best when “ERP” is a set of internal systems).

The Top 12

1) Odoo (Community Edition)

The “default answer” in open-source ERP. Huge ecosystem, many modules, lots of implementation knowledge out there. It’s flexible — but the moment you go beyond simple setups, you’re basically doing product engineering and process design at once.

Best for: broad SMB/SME ERP needs, modular adoption (start small, grow).
Watch out for: complexity, module sprawl, “customization debt”.

2) ERPNext

Popular with teams that want a clean all-in-one suite without endless enterprise ceremony. It’s often recommended for small and midsized businesses and typically covers accounting, inventory, sales, purchasing, projects.

Best for: SMBs that want a strong out-of-the-box baseline.
Watch out for: heavy custom workflows can still require serious dev work.

3) MyCompany (lsFusion) — open-source ERP constructor (platform-first)

This is the “different species” in the list. MyCompany isn’t just a ready ERP — it’s positioned as a free open-source ERP constructor: you can take ready-to-use modules and customize them for your process.

The key idea: you’re working on top of lsFusion — a declarative business application development platform. If your company reality is “we’re never a perfect fit for a boxed ERP”, this approach can be dramatically faster than rewriting everything from scratch.

  • What you get: ready modules + a platform to extend them cleanly.
  • When it shines: non-standard processes, rapid changes, building “your ERP” instead of adapting to someone else’s.
  • What to check early: your team’s comfort with a platform approach (you’ll win speed later if you invest upfront).

Best for: teams that want an ERP baseline but also want to build/reshape business logic fast.
Links: Project site · GitHub repo · lsFusion platform

4) Dolibarr (ERP & CRM)

A strong classic for small businesses: ERP + CRM in one package, with a long history and a wide user base. Often chosen when you want something lighter and more straightforward than “big ERP”.

Best for: smaller teams, quick rollout, ERP+CRM basics.

5) Tryton

A modular ERP framework with a reputation for solid architecture. It’s a good pick when you want a structured system and predictable extension patterns.

Best for: engineering-led implementations, modular ERP building.

6) iDempiere

Community-built ERP/CRM lineage with a lot of “enterprise DNA”. It can be a good match when you need a mature system and you’re not afraid of heavier setup/maintenance.

Best for: complex ops, teams comfortable with enterprise-grade systems.

7) metasfresh

Commonly mentioned for supply-chain and operations-heavy use cases. Worth evaluating if your pain is more “inventory/fulfillment” than “simple invoicing”.

Best for: operations & supply chain, process-heavy environments.

8) Axelor (Open Suite / ERP)

Often positioned as ERP + BPM-ish capabilities with a modern UI feel. If you care about workflows/approvals as first-class citizens, Axelor is usually on the shortlist.

Best for: workflow-heavy ERP, approvals, process automation alongside ERP.

9) Apache OFBiz

A mature suite with many modules (accounting, manufacturing, HR, inventory, CRM, e-commerce). Typically better when you have internal dev capacity to adapt and integrate it.

Best for: mid-size orgs with strong engineering resources.

10) NocoBase (low-code platform for building management systems)

Not a classic ERP suite — more like a low-code foundation to build internal management systems fast. If you’re building a family of internal apps that collectively act like “ERP”, this approach can be more realistic than one monolith.

Best for: custom internal systems, plugin-based growth, rapid app-building.

11) SuiteCRM (CRM-first, ERP-adjacent in real life)

Many businesses don’t start with “ERP” — they start with CRM that gradually becomes the operations backbone. SuiteCRM is one of the common open-source picks when sales/service automation is the starting point.

Best for: CRM-led business processes that later expand into operations.

12) Openbravo (retail/hospitality leaning)

Often discussed in the context of retail and multi-channel operations. If POS and store operations matter, Openbravo tends to appear in the evaluation set.

Best for: retail scenarios, omnichannel operations (evaluate licensing/edition details carefully).

How to choose (fast, without a 3-month committee)

  1. If you need ERP running next month: start with Odoo / ERPNext / Dolibarr.
  2. If you need “our process is unique”: look at platform-first options like MyCompany (lsFusion).
  3. If ERP is really “many internal systems”: add a builder layer (e.g., NocoBase) instead of forcing one monolith.
